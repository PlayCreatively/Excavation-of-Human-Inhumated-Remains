### Exposure: "Digging" without Disturbing

*   **Tools:**
    *   **Trowel:** For removing the bulk of the soil *above* the skeleton.
    *   **Wooden/Plastic tools (or spoons):** Used when getting close to bone to avoid scratching it.
    *   **Brushes:** For sweeping away loose dirt.
    *   **Dentist picks:** For fine detail (cleaning eye sockets, between ribs).

*   **Technique:**
    *   **Work horizontally:** Excavate in thin, even layers (spits) of 2‚Äì5 cm. Never dig straight down blindly.
    *   **"Pedestalling" (Avoid this!):** A common rookie mistake is digging around a bone until it sits on a little tower of dirt. This makes it unstable. *Correct method:* Keep the bone supported by dirt underneath it until you are ready to lift.
    *   **Order of Exposure:** Usually, you expose the **skull and major long bones** first to understand the position. You often leave the **hands, feet, and face** for last because they are fragile and complex (many small bones).

### Degradation
#### Clothing

- **Science:** Clothing tend to be eaten up by **bacteria**.
- **Science:** When bronze or copper corrodes, it releases toxic metal salts. These salts kill the bacteria that usually eat fabric.
- **Evidence:** If the body was wearing a bronze brooch (jewelry) or a belt buckle, the small patch of fabric directly touching that metal will be preserved.

### Grave back-filling
Grave back-filling is¬†the process of refilling an excavated grave with the previously removed soil to ensure proper closure, ground stability, and safety. The soil is usually returned in layers and compacted to prevent future sinking (settling) or erosion, often with a final mounded layer to allow for further settling.¬†

**Key Considerations for Proper Grave Back-filling:**

- **Layered Compaction:**¬†Soil should be replaced in 15cm to 20cm layers, with each layer compacted to prevent future settling.
- **Material Quality:**¬†The excavated soil should be free of large debris, rocks, and, in some cases, excessive clay, which can hinder proper compaction.
- **Drainage:**¬†Proper compaction helps ensure that water does not pool in the hole.
- **Mounding:**¬†It is common to create a slight mound of earth over the grave to compensate for the settling that will occur over time.

#### Key aspects of grave-fill boundaries include:
- **Detection Methods:**¬†Geophysical techniques like Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR) are most effective at detecting these boundaries, as they can identify differences in soil density, moisture, and compaction between the fill and the surrounding ground.
- **Physical Characteristics:**¬†The fill within a grave is typically less dense than the surrounding soil, which often results in higher moisture content.
- **Soil Composition:**¬†The fill is composed of a mixture of the original soil excavated to make the grave.
- **Archaeological Evidence:**¬†In archaeological contexts, the fill is often identified by changes in soil color or texture, indicating that the soil has been previously disturbed.
- **Process:**¬†During excavation, the boundary is identified as the edge of the pit, and maintaining the integrity of this wall is essential for proper stratigraphic recording.
